Discover Belize

Originally a quiet fishing community settled by the English in the 17th century, Placencia has blossomed into a premier eco-tourism destination. [1, 12, 16] Its name, meaning "Pleasant Point," was given by Spanish travelers. [1, 12] Today, it's known for its 16 miles of natural sandy beaches, a vibrant local culture, and the world's narrowest main street. [1, 19]

Learn More

As Belize's largest island, Ambergris Caye has a rich history dating back to the Maya civilization. [18, 5] Once a hub for European pirates and whalers, it's now a top tourist mecca, famous for its proximity to the Belize Barrier Reef, a UNESCO World Heritage Site. [3, 9] The island's only town, San Pedro, has transitioned from a sleepy fishing village to a bustling center with a casual beach-life vibe and upscale amenities. [5, 4]

Learn More

Often called the "Wild West" of Belize, the Cayo District is the country's agricultural heartland and a hub for adventure and ecotourism. [27] This region teems with wildlife in pristine rainforests and is home to over 600 ancient Mayan sites, including the magnificent cities of Caracol and Xunantunich. [26, 24] Its cultural hub, San Ignacio Town, is a vibrant mix of cultures, from Maya and Mestizo to Mennonite and Creole communities. [27, 23]

Learn More

Considered the cultural center of the Garifuna population in Belize, Hopkins is a friendly fishing village on the southern coast. [11, 10] Founded by Garifuna settlers in the early 19th century, the village maintains a proud Afro-Caribbean heritage, sharing its legacy through food, dance, and the powerful sound of drumming that often fills the air. [2, 7] Its five miles of sandy beaches and laid-back vibe make it an ideal base for exploring both the barrier reef and nearby jungle sanctuaries. [13, 15]
